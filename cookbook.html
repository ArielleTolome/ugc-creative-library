Recipe 01
🥗 Raw UGC — Authentic Discovery
Spontaneous, imperfect, FaceTime energy. The creator just found something and has to share it. Highest versatility across verticals.
Serves
Cold traffic · Awareness
Word Count
40–50 words
Energy
70–100%
Ad Signal
Medium
Face Required
Yes
Best For
All verticals
Shot Sequence
Shot 1
0–4s
Hook — Pattern interrupt or discovery
Creator mid-motion, energetic. Opener flies. No intro, no "hey guys." Handheld drift.
Shot 2
4–11s
Discovery — The thing they found + why it matters
Peer-to-peer explanation. What it is, why they're excited, the specific value. Conversational, imperfect.
Shot 3
11–15s
Share — Light recommendation + link
Friend-to-friend. Not a command. "I just wanted to share." Link is casual, not a command.
Chef's Notes
  • Slight camera drift signals it's not a professional setup
  • Background noise or activity makes it feel real
  • Creator looking slightly off-camera occasionally = thinking in real time
  • Imperfect framing — just slightly off-center is gold
  • Natural breath pauses between thoughts
Never Do
  • Never open with "Hey guys!" or any greeting
  • Never use a ring light or obviously professional lighting
  • Never read from a visible script or teleprompter
  • Never use soft, polished CTAs ("check out the link below!")
  • Never over-correct color or use heavy filters

Recipe 02
🔥 DR Hook — Pattern Interrupt Assault
Opens mid-thought. No warmup. Pattern interrupt into agitation into hard CTA. Creator is urgent, direct, almost confrontational. Best for retargeting and warm audiences.
Serves
Retargeting · Warm traffic
Word Count
42–52 words
Energy
80–100%
Ad Signal
High
Face Required
Yes
Best For
Auto, ACA, retarget
Shot Sequence
Shot 1
0–3s
Pattern Interrupt — Starts mid-sentence
Already in motion. "— and that's why you need to stop." No intro, no greeting. High energy immediately.
Shot 2
3–10s
Agitation — Problem is costing them right now
Name the specific cost or consequence. Numbers work well. Urgency builds. Creator almost frustrated on their behalf.
Shot 3
10–15s
Hard CTA — Specific action, specific reason now
Single clear action. Real reason to do it tonight. "That's it." or "I'm done." Ends strong.
Chef's Notes
  • Start mid-sentence or mid-thought — viewer was already watching something
  • Lean into camera slightly — physical intensity signals urgency
  • Specific numbers in the agitation = credibility
  • Hard stop at end, no soft close
Never Do
  • Never warm up — it starts mid-thought, always
  • Never introduce yourself
  • Never use soft CTAs or "whenever you're ready"
  • Never use hype words: amazing, incredible, life-changing
  • Never use fake scarcity ("only 3 left!")

Recipe 03
✨ Branded Lifestyle — Organic Integration
Product lives inside the creator's life. Never formally introduced. Feels like an influencer organically sharing their world. Lowest ad signal of all talking-head formats.
Serves
Cold traffic · Awareness
Word Count
38–48 words
Energy
65–80%
Ad Signal
Very Low
Face Required
Yes
Best For
ACA, cold traffic
Shot Sequence
Shot 1
0–4s
Lifestyle Hook — Creator already in their world
Showing something about their life. Not the product. Their routine, morning, activity.
Shot 2
4–11s
Natural Discovery — Product appears in their life
Product is found, not presented. "Oh by the way..." Product is part of their existing story.
Shot 3
11–15s
Casual Share — Link dropped without pressure
Soft close. Not a command. "It's in my bio if you're curious." No urgency language.
Chef's Notes
  • The lifestyle must feel authentic — not staged for the ad
  • Product discovery must feel accidental, not engineered
  • Wardrobe and setting should match the life they're showing
  • Energy is relaxed — not selling, sharing
Never Do
  • Never center the product — it lives at the edges of their life
  • Never formally introduce the product ("this is...")
  • Never use urgency language in the close
  • Never break the lifestyle illusion with sales language
  • Never use a hard CTA — link is casual, mentioned once

Recipe 04
🎤 Polished Testimonial — Credible Authority
More composed than Raw UGC. Context → Transformation → Endorsement. Slightly elevated wardrobe and framing. Best for Medicare and Final Expense where trust is the primary barrier.
Serves
Warm traffic · Trust building
Word Count
40–50 words
Energy
65–85%
Ad Signal
Medium
Face Required
Yes
Best For
Medicare, Final Expense
Shot Sequence
Shot 1
0–4s
Context — Who they are + the situation before
Brief identity and the problem or starting point. Specific details make it credible.
Shot 2
4–11s
Transformation — What happened and what they got
Specific results with numbers. Not "I saved money" — "I went from $180 to $47 a month."
Shot 3
11–15s
Endorsement — The recommendation with reason
Why they'd tell someone else. Specific. Not generic praise.
Chef's Notes
  • Sustained eye contact — looking at one person, not the world
  • Window light at 45° for clean, credible look
  • Specific numbers in transformation: amounts, timeframes, before/after
  • Wardrobe one level above casual — clean, not corporate
Never Do
  • Never use casual filler words (um, like, you know)
  • Never exceed 85% energy — this isn't hype
  • Never use hype words: amazing, incredible, game-changer
  • Never use aggressive sales close language
  • Never drift eye contact mid-endorsement

Recipe 05
↔ Before / After — Witnessed Transformation
Hard structural break between two visual states. Low energy first half (the before), elevated energy second half (the after). Lighting or location changes between halves. The contrast does the selling.
Serves
Cold + warm traffic
Word Count
38–46 words
Energy
40% → 85%
Ad Signal
Medium
Face Required
Yes
Best For
Any transformation
Shot Sequence
Before
0–7s
The Before — Low energy, visible problem
Dim lighting, tired expression, stressed body language. Creator describes the problem in present tense as if still in it.
CUT
Hard Cut — Visual break between states
Different lighting. Different position or location. The cut IS the transformation moment.
After
7–15s
The After — Elevated energy, resolved problem
Brighter, more composed. Creator describes the after in present tense. Ends with the offer and CTA.
Chef's Notes
  • The lighting change is the most important visual signal of the transformation
  • Before: slouched, tired, low voice; After: upright, energetic, confident
  • Specific numbers in the after half — amounts, timeframes
  • The cut should be abrupt, not a fade
Never Do
  • Never keep the same energy/lighting in both halves
  • Never fade between before and after — cut hard
  • Never make the before too dramatic (unbelievable)
  • Never forget that the after needs a clear result with numbers

Recipe 06
🎙 Voiceover B-Roll — Personal Documentary
No face. Ambient footage with intimate narration. Creator's voice over their daily-life footage. Feels like a personal documentary. Lowest ad signal of all formats — Facebook almost never flags it.
Serves
Cold traffic · All stages
Word Count
40–50 words
Energy
55–75%
Ad Signal
Lowest
Face Required
No
Best For
Final Expense, lifestyle
Shot Sequence
B-Roll 1
0–5s
Context footage — Their world without them in it
Kitchen, porch, family photos, hands doing things. Voiceover sets context and the problem.
B-Roll 2
5–11s
Discovery footage — Finding the solution
Phone screen, hands on keyboard, mail opening. Voiceover narrates the discovery.
B-Roll 3
11–15s
Resolution footage — Life after
Peaceful, resolved b-roll. Voiceover delivers result and soft recommendation.
Chef's Notes
  • Voiceover should sound like journaling, not presenting
  • Natural ambient sound underneath the voiceover adds depth
  • Footage should be imperfect, handheld, unstaged
  • The intimacy comes from the contrast of personal voice over public footage
Never Do
  • Never use stock footage — must feel real and personal
  • Never use a professional voice/announcer tone
  • Never remove all ambient sound — silence sounds fake
  • Never exceed 50 words — space and breath matter here

Recipe 07
👁 POV Discovery Cam — First-Person Reveal
Camera IS the creator's eyes. Continuous first-person footage. Viewer experiences the discovery as it happens. Zero ad signal — looks exactly like organic content.
Serves
Cold traffic · Discovery
Word Count
35–45 words
Energy
60–85%
Ad Signal
Very Low
Face Required
No
Best For
Price reveals, site demos
Shot Sequence
POV 1
0–4s
Stumble — Viewer enters mid-discovery
Camera is moving, searching. Creator voice narrates what they're doing/seeing. Handheld POV.
POV 2
4–11s
Reveal — The thing they found
Camera lands on the key visual — a screen, a price, a letter. Voice reacts in real time.
POV 3
11–15s
Share — "You have to see this"
Creator narrates why they're sharing. Soft link drop. No hard sell.
Chef's Notes
  • Camera movement must feel purposeful but imperfect — searching, not staged
  • Natural ambient sound = authenticity (keyboard clicks, background noise)
  • The reveal moment should feel like the viewer is discovering it too
  • Voice should react with genuine micro-emotions — small surprise, quiet excitement
Never Do
  • Never show the creator's face — camera IS their eyes
  • Never use a tripod or stabilized shot — must feel handheld
  • Never use a hard CTA close
  • Never over-narrate — let the footage do work

Recipe 08
🌿 Ambient Slice — Life In Progress
Footage already rolling when something happens. Product is present, never presented. 20–30 words maximum. Zero ad signal. The quietest ad format. Often the highest-trust cold traffic approach.
Serves
Cold traffic · Trust
Word Count
20–30 words MAX
Energy
40–65%
Ad Signal
Lowest of all
Face Required
No
Best For
Final Expense, lifestyle
Shot Sequence
Ambient 1
0–7s
Life happening — No awareness of camera
Real activity. No performance. Sound of the environment is the primary track. Creator may or may not be visible.
Ambient 2
7–12s
The mention — Casually, not addressing camera
Creator mentions the product/result in passing. As if talking to someone else in the room, not to the camera.
Ambient 3
12–15s
Return to life — No CTA, just ambient
Life continues. No close. No link. Product was just… there.
Chef's Notes
  • The camera was already running — creator never looks at it
  • Sound design is everything — birds, TV in background, kitchen sounds
  • The mention is offhand, like talking to someone else in the room
  • No CTA. Zero selling. That is the selling.
Never Do
  • Never acknowledge the camera at any point
  • Never use direct address ("you")
  • Never include a CTA of any kind
  • Never exceed 30 words of dialogue
  • Never use bright frontal lighting — must feel ambient and real

Recipe 09
😮 Reaction Cam — Witnessed Emotion
Face in frame reacting to something off-screen. No direct address until closing shot. Micro-expressions carry all the weight. Best for price and number reveals where the reaction IS the proof.
Serves
Cold + warm traffic
Word Count
25–35 words
Energy
50–75%
Ad Signal
Very Low
Face Required
Yes
Best For
Number reveals, price drops
Shot Sequence
Shot 1
0–5s
Open silent — Face reacting to off-screen stimulus
No words. Face changes. Eyes widen, jaw drops, head tilts. Viewer is curious what they're seeing.
Shot 2
5–11s
Verbal reaction — Still looking off-screen
"Wait. That can't be right." Short reactive phrases. Still not addressing the camera directly.
Shot 3
11–15s
Turn to camera — Share the moment
First direct address. "You need to see this." One-line recommendation. Soft link drop.
Chef's Notes
  • The opening silent reaction is everything — it creates the question "what are they looking at?"
  • Reactions must be natural, not theatrical
  • Less words = more impact for this format
  • The turn to camera at end feels like an invitation, not a pitch
Never Do
  • Never open with dialogue — silence first, always
  • Never make eye contact with camera until closing shot
  • Never over-direct the reaction — it must feel unplanned
  • Never exceed 35 words total
  • Never use a hard CTA — one line, soft recommendation only

Recipe 10
🎬 UGC Testimonial On-Camera — The Full Stack
The most complete format. Combines the best of Raw UGC authenticity with the structure of Polished Testimonial. Hook → Story → Evidence → Recommendation. The workhorse of insurance lead gen.
Serves
All stages · All verticals
Word Count
42–52 words
Energy
70–90%
Ad Signal
Medium
Face Required
Yes
Best For
All 4 verticals
Shot Sequence
Shot 1
0–4s
Hook — Stops the scroll
Pattern interrupt, bold question, or identity call-out. No greeting.
Shot 2
4–9s
Story + Evidence — What happened
Specific numbers, specific timeline, specific result. Not "I saved money" — exact amounts.
Shot 3
9–15s
Recommendation + CTA
Why they're sharing. One clear action. Real reason to do it now.
Chef's Notes
  • The hook is the whole game — if it doesn't stop the scroll, nothing else matters
  • Specificity = credibility: name numbers, timelines, before/after amounts
  • The recommendation should feel like advice to a friend, not a sales pitch
  • Creator's setting should match their character — real life, not a backdrop
Never Do
  • Never start with a greeting or intro
  • Never make vague claims without numbers
  • Never end with a weak close ("check it out maybe")
  • Never use hype vocabulary
  • Never run over 52 words

Body Styles — 50 Middle-of-Ad Structures
How the middle of the ad is structured. Hook got them watching. Body keeps them watching. Choose one.
01
Problem / Solution
Present the problem clearly, introduce the solution. Simple, clean, logical. Works in any vertical.
02
Personal Narrative
Story with beginning, middle, end. Viewer is transported. Empathy-based persuasion.
03
Feature → Benefit Translation
Walk through features, translate each into a viewer benefit. Not what it does — what it means for their life.
04
Testimonial / Personal Proof
Specific results with numbers, timelines, before/after states. Specificity is the credibility.
05
Step-by-Step Process
Break down how to take action in numbered steps. Reduces perceived complexity. Simplicity is the product.
06
Before / After Contrast
Life before vs. life after. The contrast does the selling.
07
Live Demonstration
Show the product in action — screen recordings, physical demos. Seeing = believing.
08
Objection Handling Sequence
Name and overcome the 2–3 most likely reasons NOT to act. Pre-empt the "but..."
09
Emotional Journey Arc
Frustration → hope → resolution. Product is the emotional turning point.
10
Competitor Comparison
This vs. the alternative. Always frame this solution as the clear winner.
11
Educational Explanation
Teach something valuable. Viewer feels informed, not sold. Authority through giving value first.
12
Multiple Benefits List
Enumerate several key advantages rapidly. Value accumulation: "all of this? for that?"
13
Discovery Reveal Walk-Through
Share how you found the solution. The journey of finding it makes the product feel earned, not sold.
14
Social Proof Compilation
Stack proof elements — reviews, numbers, results. Volume of proof creates credibility.
15
Expert Breakdown
Explain with authority why it works. Technical enough to be credible, accessible enough to use.
16
Pain Amplification
Deepen the problem before presenting the solution. Deeper pain = more valuable the relief.
17
Transformation Story
Complete arc — where you were, what changed, where you are now. Not just the result.
18
Case Study — One Person
"My mom, 67, from [city]..." Specificity makes it believable. One story beats generic claims.
19
How-To Tutorial
Walk through the exact process viewer needs to take. Action-forward, practical, empowering.
20
Urgency / Scarcity Build
Real reasons why waiting costs something. Not fake countdown — actual stakes.
21
Myth-Busting Deep Dive
"You think [wrong belief] but actually [truth]." Frees viewer from false constraint.
22
Behind-the-Scenes Look
Insider perspective not normally visible. Feeling of exclusive access even in a public ad.
23
Vulnerability / Confession
Share struggles and breakthrough. Being imperfect and honest is the persuasion.
24
Logic / Rational Argument
Systematic case with data and cause-effect. For analytical decision-makers.
25
Sensory / Experiential Description
Paint a vivid picture of the experience. Activates imagination before action.
26
Risk Reversal
"What's the worst that could happen? You check and it takes 90 seconds." Reduce the downside.
27
Authority Stacking
Layer credibility — credentials, years, specific results. Stack until doubt dissolves.
28
Common Mistakes Breakdown
Name the specific errors viewer is probably making right now. Naming their mistakes builds trust.
29
Future-Pacing Vision
Detailed picture of life after taking action. Mind begins wanting what it can clearly picture.
30
Relatability Building
"I know you know this feeling..." Identification precedes persuasion.
31
Value Stacking
Accumulate multiple value propositions until the offer feels undeniable.
32
Simplicity Emphasis
"You just enter your zip. That's it." Every complexity removed lowers the action barrier.
33
Investment Justification
Explain why cost or effort is worth it. Compare to what they already spend on less important things.
34
Exclusivity / FOMO
"This is only for people who [qualifier]." Exclusivity creates desire even for broadly available offers.
35
Community / Belonging
"Thousands in [state] already did this." Social belonging is a fundamental driver.
36
Challenge Overcome
How a specific difficulty was conquered. Creator's challenge mirrors viewer's hesitation.
37
Results Documentation
Show proof through specific numbers or evidence. Not claims — receipts.
38
Lifestyle Integration
Demonstrate how product fits naturally into daily life. Not a purchase — an upgrade to an existing routine.
39
Permission Granting
"You're allowed to want a better rate." Remove the psychological permission barrier.
40
The Ripple Effect
"When I got this covered, I stopped worrying, slept better, showed up better." Chain of benefits beyond obvious.
41
The Peer Explanation
"Okay so basically what it does is..." No jargon. How you'd explain it at a barbecue.
42
The Industry Exposé
Reveal how the industry works against the consumer. Offer = loophole or escape route.
43
The Time Machine
"If I could tell past-me one thing about [topic]..." Retroactive wisdom. Act now so future-you has no regrets.
44
The Quiet Relief
Not excitement of finding a deal — the quiet weight that lifted. Peace of mind as the product.
45
The Speed Run
Rapid-fire benefits at high pace. Density = credibility. So much value you can't cover it all.
46
The Anchor and Reframe
Name the high price first, reveal actual price. Contrast makes the real number feel like a steal.
47
The Generational Obligation
Frame the decision as something you do for family. Moves from self-interest to legacy and love.
48
The Relief of Certainty
Sells the removal of ongoing low-grade stress. Not features or price — just not having to worry anymore.
49
The Earned Trust Close
Entire body builds trust through vulnerability, specificity, or humor — so CTA lands like a friend's advice.
50
The Skeptic's Journey
Voice the viewer's own doubt, then walk through the process of overcoming it. Pre-handles objection before they have it.

How to Build an Input Prompt — Step by Step
The input prompt is a single paragraph that gives the video generation system everything it needs. Follow this formula.
Field 1
[Age + Ethnicity + Gender]
Be specific. "63 year old Black woman" not "older woman." Age range OK: "45-52 year old."
Field 2
[Accent]
Always specific. "warm Georgia accent" not "Southern accent." Pull from the Accent Library.
Field 3
[Wardrobe — 3 specific items]
"wearing a comfortable floral blouse, reading glasses, and a cardigan" — three specific pieces minimum.
Field 4
[Location + Action — specific]
"sitting at her kitchen table with bills and family photos on the wall behind her" — location + what they're doing + what's in the environment.
Field 5
[Camera Notes — optional but powerful]
"handheld medium frame with slight natural drift" or "locked-off medium shot with window light from left" — tells the system how to shoot it.
Field 6
[Energy + Physical Behavior]
"genuine relieved energy at 72%" or "direct intense eye contact at 88%, leaning slightly toward camera."
Field 7
saying "[Hook + Body + CTA as natural dialogue]"
Write it as natural speech, not ad copy. Hook → discovery/evidence → offer → CTA. No bullet points — one flowing natural statement.
Complete Example
"63 year old Black woman with a warm Georgia accent wearing a comfortable floral blouse and reading glasses sitting at her kitchen table with bills and family photos on the wall behind her, handheld medium frame with slight natural drift, genuine relieved energy at 72%, saying "I was sitting here stressing about what my kids would have to deal with when I'm gone and I found this. No medical exam. I qualified immediately. Starting at eighteen dollars a month my family doesn't have to handle a thing. Link's in the comments.""
