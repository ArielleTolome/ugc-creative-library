Section 01
Testing Priority Order

Test in this order. Each level has lower expected impact than the one above it. Don't skip to wardrobe tests when you haven't exhausted hook variations. Start at the top of the stack.

1
Hook — First 3 Seconds
The single highest-impact variable in ad creative. The hook determines whether anyone watches the rest of the ad. Test 5+ hook variations before moving to anything else. Different first lines, different emotional registers, different delivery speed.
Highest Impact
2
Format / Style
Raw UGC vs Polished Testimonial vs DR Hook vs Ambient Slice. Same hook, different energy and production style. This answers whether your vertical responds to authenticity (raw) or authority (polished) or information (DR).
Very High Impact
3
Character Angle
Occupation vs life event vs skeptic arc vs community discovery. Same format and hook, different character identity. FE: church community vs professional identity. Auto: suburban parent vs young driver. ACA: freelancer vs gig worker.
High Impact
4
Offer Frame
Specific number ($22/month) vs general ("affordable coverage") vs no number at all. Insurance verticals almost always prefer specific numbers. But test the frame — "from $22" vs "as low as $22" vs just "$22" perform differently.
High Impact
5
CTA Style
Soft ("check it out") vs urgent ("do it tonight") vs benefit-forward ("see your rate in 7 minutes"). The CTA is a micro-hook at the end. Test length, specificity, and urgency level independently.
Medium Impact
6
Wardrobe / Visual Signals
Signals character identity without a word. Sunday clothes (church, community) vs business casual (professional authority) vs flannel (authentic, relatable). Test wardrobe changes when you've found a winning character angle.
Medium Impact
7
Location / Setting
Porch vs kitchen vs car vs home office. Setting does subconscious credibility work. FE: home settings outperform blank walls. Auto: car interior or parking lot beats generic homes. Medicare: kitchen table with visible paperwork.
Lower Impact
8
Accent / Character Identity
Accent must match the character's stated background. Southern character needs Southern delivery. This isn't a test variable so much as a quality control check — mismatches kill performance. Only test accent when you've confirmed the character works.
Quality Control
Section 02
Hook A/B Test Pairs
10 battle-tested pairs

Each pair is a real test with a real recommendation. Green = Version B usually wins. Blue = Version A usually wins. Yellow = context-dependent. Run these with identical body copy — only change the first line.

01 Direct Stat vs Question Hook B Wins
Version A — Statement
"The average American funeral costs $11,847."
Version B — Question
"Does your family have $11,847 liquid right now?"
🏆 B usually wins because the question creates personal stake. The viewer stops scrolling to mentally answer it. A is a fact; B is a challenge. In FE especially, the interrogative form forces self-qualification.
02 Broad Appeal vs Specific Identity B Wins
Version A — Broad
"Anyone who hasn't checked their car insurance recently..."
Version B — Specific
"If you've been with the same insurance company for more than 3 years..."
🏆 B wins via specificity = self-selection. The "3 years" threshold causes the viewer to immediately calculate how long they've been with their insurer. Self-qualified viewers convert at 2–3x the rate of broad appeals.
03 Problem Hook vs Solution Hook Context Dependent
Version A — Problem
"Original Medicare doesn't cover everything."
Version B — Solution
"Zero-premium Medicare plans exist. Here's who qualifies."
⚖️ Funnel-stage dependent. Cold traffic: A wins — the problem hook interrupts the scroll by activating fear. Warm retargeting: B wins — they already know the problem, they want to know if the solution applies to them. Run A on top-of-funnel audiences; B on retargeting.
04 First-Person Confession vs Third-Person Advice B Wins
Version A — Third Person
"If you're freelancing without health insurance..."
Version B — First Person
"I was uninsured for two years doing this freelance thing..."
🏆 First-person wins on trust. In UGC, the audience's trust detection is very high. Third-person advice sounds like an ad. First-person confession sounds like a person. The "I" creates parasocial credibility that the "you" framing can't replicate.
05 Specific Number vs Emotional Opener Traffic-Stage Dependent
Version A — Number
"Twenty-two dollars a month."
Version B — Emotional
"I wasn't going to share this but I've been asked too many times not to."
⚖️ Cold traffic: A wins — the specific number stops the scroll because the brain needs to process it. Warm retargeting: B wins — emotional curiosity gap hooks people who've already been exposed to the offer. Test both on cold; use B to refresh fatigued audiences.
06 Urgency/Deadline vs No Urgency Date Dependent
Version A — Deadline
"ACA enrollment closes January 15th. That's 12 days."
Version B — Evergreen
"ACA coverage is available to anyone self-employed."
⚖️ Date proximity determines winner. When the deadline is under 30 days away: A wins significantly — urgency is the conversion driver. When the deadline is far away or no deadline exists: B wins — false urgency collapses trust. Only run deadline hooks when the deadline is real and close.
07 High Energy vs Low Energy Vertical Dependent
Version A — High Energy
DR Hook format · 90% energy · Fast delivery · Forward lean
Version B — Low Energy
Polished Testimonial · 60% energy · Measured pace · Composed
⚖️ Vertical determines winner. Auto / ACA: A wins — younger demos and comparison-shopper mindsets respond to urgency and direct delivery. Final Expense / Medicare: B wins — older demos trust composed authority over excited energy. Mismatching energy to vertical is a common creative killer (see Section 4, #1).
08 Professional Credential vs Peer Testimonial B Wins
Version A — Professional
"I work in insurance and I'm telling you to compare your rate."
Version B — Peer
"I compared my insurance and saved $94 a month."
🏆 Peer credibility wins in UGC. The audience is trained to distrust professional endorsements in paid content. A peer who "just figured it out" is more trustworthy than a professional who "knows better." Exception: Medicare, where advisor framing with "they're paid by the carrier, not you" can outperform peer on some audiences.
09 Generic CTA vs Specific CTA B Wins
Version A — Generic
"Click the link below."
Version B — Specific
"Tap the link — it takes you straight to the comparison. Takes seven minutes."
🏆 Specificity reduces friction uncertainty. The viewer's hesitation at the CTA is "what happens when I tap?" B answers that question before they ask it. "Seven minutes" is the specific commitment — it removes the fear of a long sales process. Always name the time commitment in the CTA.
10 Single CTA vs Double CTA B Wins
Version A — Single
"Link below."
Version B — Double
"Link below, or comment your zip code and I'll send you the link directly."
🏆 Double CTA creates engagement signal + backup path. The comment CTA converts non-clickers into leads via DM/comment reply automation. On Meta specifically, comment engagement boosts organic distribution of the ad. The zip code ask is a micro-commitment that pre-qualifies for geo-specific insurance offers.
Section 03
Testing Velocity Framework

How many tests to run, how long to let them run, when to kill, when to scale. These are thresholds for insurance verticals on Meta. Adjust for other platforms.

Min Budget Per Variant
$50–$200
Before reading results. Lower-funnel offers (phone calls, form fills) need $200+. Higher-funnel (calculator, quiz) can signal at $50–100.
Min Impressions Before Reading
2,000
Per variant. Reading at 500 impressions is noise. At 2K, CTR patterns are directionally reliable. Statistical significance requires more — but you're making directional calls, not academic ones.
Kill Threshold
<0.5% CTR
At 1,000 impressions. If a hook can't generate 0.5% CTR on cold traffic in 1K impressions, it's not going to magically improve with more spend. Kill it.
Scale Threshold
>2.5% CTR
OR cost per lead below target CPA. Both signals together = winner. CTR alone isn't enough — verify that the lead quality from the conversion is acceptable before scaling budget.
Weekly Velocity Target
3–5
New hook variants per week, plus 1 new character angle every 2–3 weeks. More than 5 hooks/week and the signal-to-noise ratio collapses — you can't evaluate properly.
Promote Timeline
72hr → 7d
72-hour initial read to kill obvious losers. 7-day full evaluation to promote winners. Never scale based on less than 3 days of data — early spikes are often algorithm-dependent noise.
Testing Rules of Thumb
→Never change more than one variable per A/B test. Change the hook OR the format, not both.
→Run variants in the same ad set at the same time. Different ad sets introduce targeting variance that corrupts the test.
→Document what you kill and why. The kill log is as valuable as the winner log. It tells you what this audience doesn't respond to.
→Don't run more than 5 variants at once. Each variant needs adequate budget to reach threshold. Spreading too thin = invalid results.
→80% kill rate is healthy. If you're promoting more than 2 out of 10 hooks, your quality control on hook writing is too loose.
Section 04
What Kills an Ad
10 creative killers

These are the things that kill performance even when the hook is good, the script is right, and the offer is solid. Use this as a pre-flight checklist before any ad goes live.

01
Mismatched Energy
90% DR Hook delivery energy on a testimonial script. Or a 55% polished energy on a DR Hook script. Viewers' trust detectors are calibrated to energy-content alignment. If the delivery energy contradicts what the script is saying, the viewer clocks it as "off" and disengages — even if they can't explain why. Match energy to format, always.
02
Wrong Accent for the Character
PNW flat delivery on a Deep South character. Upper-Midwest accent on a Brooklyn-coded script. Accent mismatches break parasocial believability instantly. The viewer's brain pattern-matches on accent and background before processing content. Disconnect = distrust. Casting must match the character's claimed geographic and cultural identity.
03
Too-Clean Production on Raw UGC
Tripod + professional lighting + clean audio on a "Raw UGC" testimonial. The audience knows what Raw UGC looks like. When a "raw" testimonial is shot with a cinema lens and three-point lighting, it reads as paid — which destroys the entire credibility premise. Raw UGC should look like it was shot on a phone by a person in a parking lot. Because it should be.
04
No Number in the Hook for Comparison Verticals
Auto Insurance and Medicare require a specific number in or near the hook. "I saved money on car insurance" performs significantly worse than "I saved $94 a month on car insurance." Without the number, the brain has nothing to anchor the claim. Specific numbers pass through the scroll-filter; vague claims don't.
05
Asking for Too Much in the CTA
Phone call CTA on cold traffic in comparison or calculator verticals. Cold traffic will fill a form or run a calculator. They will not call a number they don't know. The CTA commitment level must match the temperature of the audience. Cold: link/form/calculator. Warm: call. Retargeting: call or chat.
06
Background Inconsistent with Character
Beach background for a claimed hospital worker. New York City skyline for a character claiming to be from rural Georgia. The background is part of the character's credibility package. If the setting contradicts the character's stated identity, the subconscious trust score collapses. Location must support the character's life, not contradict it.
07
Wardrobe Mismatch
Authority figure script with casual wardrobe — or vice versa. In FE, the "community elder" character needs wardrobe that signals stability and care (button shirt, no logos). In Auto DR Hook, business casual signals professional knowledge. In Raw UGC, anything too polished undermines the authenticity premise. Dress the character, not the creator.
08
Too Long on Cold Traffic
45-second scripts losing to 15-second scripts on cold audiences. Cold viewers haven't committed to the creator yet. They're deciding in the first 3 seconds whether to stop and watch. A tighter 15-second version of the same hook-body-CTA almost always outperforms the longer version on initial cold traffic. Use longer formats for retargeting after awareness is established.
09
Vague Offer
No price, no number, no specific outcome. "Affordable insurance" — for what? "Save money" — how much? The viewer doesn't know what they're clicking for, so they don't click. Insurance verticals require a specific offer artifact: a price ($22/month), a savings number ($94 less), a time commitment (seven minutes), or a concrete outcome (zero premium). Vague = low CTR.
10
Fake Urgency
Obvious false scarcity: "This offer expires tonight!" when it doesn't, or "Only 3 spots left!" for an online insurance comparison. The audience has seen every fake urgency trick. When detected — and it's usually detected — trust collapses completely. The ad is rejected not just ignored. Only run deadline language when the deadline is real (AEP, OEP), specific (December 7), and close (within 30 days).
Section 05
The Creative Loop · Rachel → Marcus

The weekly cycle that keeps creative output high and waste low. Rachel produces, Marcus runs, data comes back, Rachel iterates. One loop per week. One winner per week is a good week.

Week 1
Rachel Produces
10
Variants
5 hook variations × 2 formats = 10 variants. Same character, same vertical. Brief includes full script, director notes, character specs, and energy level. Delivered to Marcus by end of week.
Week 2
Marcus Runs
72hr
Initial Read
All 10 variants run with equal budget. 72-hour initial read: report CTR and CPL per variant. No early scaling — wait for the data. Marcus flags anything below kill threshold immediately.
Week 3
Rachel Iterates
80%
Kill Rate
Kill the bottom 7–8. Study the top 2–3 for signal: what specific words, what delivery energy, what offer frame worked. Produce 5 new hooks that iterate on the winner's attributes. One new character angle introduced.
Week 4
Scale Winner
1
Winner / Week
Scale the proven winner. Introduce the next character angle into the test pool. The winner becomes the "control" — all future tests must beat it to justify budget. One winner per week is a healthy, sustainable output.
80% kill rate is healthy, not a failure
1 winner per week = strong creative operation
Data from Marcus drives Rachel's next brief
Never scale without 7-day full evaluation
Reading the Signal — What Marcus Sends Rachel
CTR >2.5%
Winner signal. Scale budget 2x. Study the hook for what's driving it. Produce 3 direct iterations on this hook's pattern.
CTR 1–2.5%
Contender. Keep running through 7-day evaluation. If CPL is acceptable, continue. If CPL is too high despite decent CTR, the landing page may be the problem, not the creative.
CTR 0.5–1%
Below benchmark but alive. Let it run to 2K impressions. If it doesn't improve, kill it. Document what it got wrong in hook delivery or format.
CTR <0.5%
Kill at 1K impressions. Note the specific failure mode: bad hook? wrong energy? wrong character? The kill reason informs Rachel's next brief as much as the winner signal.
