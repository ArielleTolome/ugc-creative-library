Recipe 01
🥗 Raw UGC — Authentic Discovery
Spontaneous, imperfect, FaceTime energy. The creator just found something and has to share it. Highest versatility across verticals.
Serves
Cold traffic · Awareness
Word Count
40–50 words
Energy
70–100%
Ad Signal
Medium
Face Required
Yes
Best For
All verticals
Shot Sequence
Shot 1
0–4s
Hook — Pattern interrupt or discovery
Creator mid-motion, energetic. Opener flies. No intro, no "hey guys." Handheld drift.
Shot 2
4–11s
Discovery — The thing they found + why it matters
Peer-to-peer explanation. What it is, why they're excited, the specific value. Conversational, imperfect.
Shot 3
11–15s
Share — Light recommendation + link
Friend-to-friend. Not a command. "I just wanted to share." Link is casual, not a command.
Chef's Notes
  • Slight camera drift signals it's not a professional setup
  • Background noise or activity makes it feel real
  • Creator looking slightly off-camera occasionally = thinking in real time
  • Imperfect framing — just slightly off-center is gold
  • Natural breath pauses between thoughts
Never Do
  • Never open with "Hey guys!" or any greeting
  • Never use a ring light or obviously professional lighting
  • Never read from a visible script or teleprompter
  • Never use soft, polished CTAs ("check out the link below!")
  • Never over-correct color or use heavy filters

Recipe 02
🔥 DR Hook — Pattern Interrupt Assault
Opens mid-thought. No warmup. Pattern interrupt into agitation into hard CTA. Creator is urgent, direct, almost confrontational. Best for retargeting and warm audiences.
Serves
Retargeting · Warm traffic
Word Count
42–52 words
Energy
80–100%
Ad Signal
High
Face Required
Yes
Best For
Auto, ACA, retarget
Shot Sequence
Shot 1
0–3s
Pattern Interrupt — Starts mid-sentence
Already in motion. "— and that's why you need to stop." No intro, no greeting. High energy immediately.
Shot 2
3–10s
Agitation — Problem is costing them right now
Name the specific cost or consequence. Numbers work well. Urgency builds. Creator almost frustrated on their behalf.
Shot 3
10–15s
Hard CTA — Specific action, specific reason now
Single clear action. Real reason to do it tonight. "That's it." or "I'm done." Ends strong.
Chef's Notes
  • Start mid-sentence or mid-thought — viewer was already watching something
  • Lean into camera slightly — physical intensity signals urgency
  • Specific numbers in the agitation = credibility
  • Hard stop at end, no soft close
Never Do
  • Never warm up — it starts mid-thought, always
  • Never introduce yourself
  • Never use soft CTAs or "whenever you're ready"
  • Never use hype words: amazing, incredible, life-changing
  • Never use fake scarcity ("only 3 left!")

Recipe 03
✨ Branded Lifestyle — Organic Integration
Product lives inside the creator's life. Never formally introduced. Feels like an influencer organically sharing their world. Lowest ad signal of all talking-head formats.
Serves
Cold traffic · Awareness
Word Count
38–48 words
Energy
65–80%
Ad Signal
Very Low
Face Required
Yes
Best For
ACA, cold traffic
Shot Sequence
Shot 1
0–4s
Lifestyle Hook — Creator already in their world
Showing something about their life. Not the product. Their routine, morning, activity.
Shot 2
4–11s
Natural Discovery — Product appears in their life
Product is found, not presented. "Oh by the way..." Product is part of their existing story.
Shot 3
11–15s
Casual Share — Link dropped without pressure
Soft close. Not a command. "It's in my bio if you're curious." No urgency language.
Chef's Notes
  • The lifestyle must feel authentic — not staged for the ad
  • Product discovery must feel accidental, not engineered
  • Wardrobe and setting should match the life they're showing
  • Energy is relaxed — not selling, sharing
Never Do
  • Never center the product — it lives at the edges of their life
  • Never formally introduce the product ("this is...")
  • Never use urgency language in the close
  • Never break the lifestyle illusion with sales language
  • Never use a hard CTA — link is casual, mentioned once

Recipe 04
🎤 Polished Testimonial — Credible Authority
More composed than Raw UGC. Context → Transformation → Endorsement. Slightly elevated wardrobe and framing. Best for Medicare and Final Expense where trust is the primary barrier.
Serves
Warm traffic · Trust building
Word Count
40–50 words
Energy
65–85%
Ad Signal
Medium
Face Required
Yes
Best For
Medicare, Final Expense
Shot Sequence
Shot 1
0–4s
Context — Who they are + the situation before
Brief identity and the problem or starting point. Specific details make it credible.
Shot 2
4–11s
Transformation — What happened and what they got
Specific results with numbers. Not "I saved money" — "I went from $180 to $47 a month."
Shot 3
11–15s
Endorsement — The recommendation with reason
Why they'd tell someone else. Specific. Not generic praise.
Chef's Notes
  • Sustained eye contact — looking at one person, not the world
  • Window light at 45° for clean, credible look
  • Specific numbers in transformation: amounts, timeframes, before/after
  • Wardrobe one level above casual — clean, not corporate
Never Do
  • Never use casual filler words (um, like, you know)
  • Never exceed 85% energy — this isn't hype
  • Never use hype words: amazing, incredible, game-changer
  • Never use aggressive sales close language
  • Never drift eye contact mid-endorsement

Recipe 05
↔ Before / After — Witnessed Transformation
Hard structural break between two visual states. Low energy first half (the before), elevated energy second half (the after). Lighting or location changes between halves. The contrast does the selling.
Serves
Cold + warm traffic
Word Count
38–46 words
Energy
40% → 85%
Ad Signal
Medium
Face Required
Yes
Best For
Any transformation
Shot Sequence
Before
0–7s
The Before — Low energy, visible problem
Dim lighting, tired expression, stressed body language. Creator describes the problem in present tense as if still in it.
CUT
Hard Cut — Visual break between states
Different lighting. Different position or location. The cut IS the transformation moment.
After
7–15s
The After — Elevated energy, resolved problem
Brighter, more composed. Creator describes the after in present tense. Ends with the offer and CTA.
Chef's Notes
  • The lighting change is the most important visual signal of the transformation
  • Before: slouched, tired, low voice; After: upright, energetic, confident
  • Specific numbers in the after half — amounts, timeframes
  • The cut should be abrupt, not a fade
Never Do
  • Never keep the same energy/lighting in both halves
  • Never fade between before and after — cut hard
  • Never make the before too dramatic (unbelievable)
  • Never forget that the after needs a clear result with numbers

Recipe 06
🎙 Voiceover B-Roll — Personal Documentary
No face. Ambient footage with intimate narration. Creator's voice over their daily-life footage. Feels like a personal documentary. Lowest ad signal of all formats — Facebook almost never flags it.
Serves
Cold traffic · All stages
Word Count
40–50 words
Energy
55–75%
Ad Signal
Lowest
Face Required
No
Best For
Final Expense, lifestyle
Shot Sequence
B-Roll 1
0–5s
Context footage — Their world without them in it
Kitchen, porch, family photos, hands doing things. Voiceover sets context and the problem.
B-Roll 2
5–11s
Discovery footage — Finding the solution
Phone screen, hands on keyboard, mail opening. Voiceover narrates the discovery.
B-Roll 3
11–15s
Resolution footage — Life after
Peaceful, resolved b-roll. Voiceover delivers result and soft recommendation.
Chef's Notes
  • Voiceover should sound like journaling, not presenting
  • Natural ambient sound underneath the voiceover adds depth
  • Footage should be imperfect, handheld, unstaged
  • The intimacy comes from the contrast of personal voice over public footage
Never Do
  • Never use stock footage — must feel real and personal
  • Never use a professional voice/announcer tone
  • Never remove all ambient sound — silence sounds fake
  • Never exceed 50 words — space and breath matter here

👁 POV Discovery Cam — First-Person Reveal
Camera IS the creator's eyes. Continuous first-person footage. Viewer experiences the discovery as it happens. Zero ad signal — looks exactly like organic content.
Serves
Cold traffic · Discovery
Word Count
35–45 words
Energy
60–85%
Ad Signal
Very Low
Face Required
No
Best For
Price reveals, site demos
Shot Sequence
POV 1
0–4s
Stumble — Viewer enters mid-discovery
Camera is moving, searching. Creator voice narrates what they're doing/seeing. Handheld POV.
POV 2
4–11s
Reveal — The thing they found
Camera lands on the key visual — a screen, a price, a letter. Voice reacts in real time.
POV 3
11–15s
Share — "You have to see this"
Creator narrates why they're sharing. Soft link drop. No hard sell.
Chef's Notes
  • Camera movement must feel purposeful but imperfect — searching, not staged
  • Natural ambient sound = authenticity (keyboard clicks, background noise)
  • The reveal moment should feel like the viewer is discovering it too
  • Voice should react with genuine micro-emotions — small surprise, quiet excitement
Never Do
  • Never show the creator's face — camera IS their eyes
  • Never use a tripod or stabilized shot — must feel handheld
  • Never use a hard CTA close
  • Never over-narrate — let the footage do work

Recipe 08
🌿 Ambient Slice — Life In Progress
Footage already rolling when something happens. Product is present, never presented. 20–30 words maximum. Zero ad signal. The quietest ad format. Often the highest-trust cold traffic approach.
Serves
Cold traffic · Trust
Word Count
20–30 words MAX
Energy
40–65%
Ad Signal
Lowest of all
Face Required
No
Best For
Final Expense, lifestyle
Shot Sequence
Ambient 1
0–7s
Life happening — No awareness of camera
Real activity. No performance. Sound of the environment is the primary track. Creator may or may not be visible.
Ambient 2
7–12s
The mention — Casually, not addressing camera
Creator mentions the product/result in passing. As if talking to someone else in the room, not to the camera.
Ambient 3
12–15s
Return to life — No CTA, just ambient
Life continues. No close. No link. Product was just… there.
Chef's Notes
  • The camera was already running — creator never looks at it
  • Sound design is everything — birds, TV in background, kitchen sounds
  • The mention is offhand, like talking to someone else in the room
  • No CTA. Zero selling. That is the selling.
Never Do
  • Never acknowledge the camera at any point
  • Never use direct address ("you")
  • Never include a CTA of any kind
  • Never exceed 30 words of dialogue
  • Never use bright frontal lighting — must feel ambient and real

Recipe 09
😮 Reaction Cam — Witnessed Emotion
Face in frame reacting to something off-screen. No direct address until closing shot. Micro-expressions carry all the weight. Best for price and number reveals where the reaction IS the proof.
Serves
Cold + warm traffic
Word Count
25–35 words
Energy
50–75%
Ad Signal
Very Low
Face Required
Yes
Best For
Number reveals, price drops
Shot Sequence
Shot 1
0–5s
Open silent — Face reacting to off-screen stimulus
No words. Face changes. Eyes widen, jaw drops, head tilts. Viewer is curious what they're seeing.
Shot 2
5–11s
Verbal reaction — Still looking off-screen
"Wait. That can't be right." Short reactive phrases. Still not addressing the camera directly.
Shot 3
11–15s
Turn to camera — Share the moment
First direct address. "You need to see this." One-line recommendation. Soft link drop.
Chef's Notes
  • The opening silent reaction is everything — it creates the question "what are they looking at?"
  • Reactions must be natural, not theatrical
  • Less words = more impact for this format
  • The turn to camera at end feels like an invitation, not a pitch
Never Do
  • Never open with dialogue — silence first, always
  • Never make eye contact with camera until closing shot
  • Never over-direct the reaction — it must feel unplanned
  • Never exceed 35 words total
  • Never use a hard CTA — one line, soft recommendation only

Recipe 10
🎬 UGC Testimonial On-Camera — The Full Stack
The most complete format. Combines the best of Raw UGC authenticity with the structure of Polished Testimonial. Hook → Story → Evidence → Recommendation. The workhorse of insurance lead gen.
Serves
All stages · All verticals
Word Count
42–52 words
Energy
70–90%
Ad Signal
Medium
Face Required
Yes
Best For
All 4 verticals
Shot Sequence
Shot 1
0–4s
Hook — Stops the scroll
Pattern interrupt, bold question, or identity call-out. No greeting.
Shot 2
4–9s
Story + Evidence — What happened
Specific numbers, specific timeline, specific result. Not "I saved money" — exact amounts.
Shot 3
9–15s
Recommendation + CTA
Why they're sharing. One clear action. Real reason to do it now.
Chef's Notes
  • The hook is the whole game — if it doesn't stop the scroll, nothing else matters
  • Specificity = credibility: name numbers, timelines, before/after amounts
  • The recommendation should feel like advice to a friend, not a sales pitch
  • Creator's setting should match their character — real life, not a backdrop
Never Do
  • Never start with a greeting or intro
  • Never make vague claims without numbers
  • Never end with a weak close ("check it out maybe")
  • Never use hype vocabulary
  • Never run over 52 words

Body Styles — 50 Middle-of-Ad Structures
How the middle of the ad is structured. Hook got them watching. Body keeps them watching. Choose one.
01
Problem / Solution
Present the problem clearly, introduce the solution. Simple, clean, logical. Works in any vertical.
02
Personal Narrative
Story with beginning, middle, end. Viewer is transported. Empathy-based persuasion.
03
Feature → Benefit Translation
Walk through features, translate each into a viewer benefit. Not what it does — what it means for their life.
04
Testimonial / Personal Proof
Specific results with numbers, timelines, before/after states. Specificity is the credibility.
05
Step-by-Step Process
Break down how to take action in numbered steps. Reduces perceived complexity. Simplicity is the product.
06
Before / After Contrast
Life before vs. life after. The contrast does the selling.
07
Live Demonstration
Show the product in action — screen recordings, physical demos. Seeing = believing.
08
Objection Handling Sequence
Name and overcome the 2–3 most likely reasons NOT to act. Pre-empt the "but..."
09
Emotional Journey Arc
Frustration → hope → resolution. Product is the emotional turning point.
10
Competitor Comparison
This vs. the alternative. Always frame this solution as the clear winner.
11
Educational Explanation
Teach something valuable. Viewer feels informed, not sold. Authority through giving value first.
12
Multiple Benefits List
Enumerate several key advantages rapidly. Value accumulation: "all of this? for that?"
13
Discovery Reveal Walk-Through
Share how you found the solution. The journey of finding it makes the product feel earned, not sold.
14
Social Proof Compilation
Stack proof elements — reviews, numbers, results. Volume of proof creates credibility.
15
Expert Breakdown
Explain with authority why it works. Technical enough to be credible, accessible enough to use.
16
Pain Amplification
Deepen the problem before presenting the solution. Deeper pain = more valuable the relief.
17
Transformation Story
Complete arc — where you were, what changed, where you are now. Not just the result.
18
Case Study — One Person
"My mom, 67, from [city]..." Specificity makes it believable. One story beats generic claims.
19
How-To Tutorial
Walk through the exact process viewer needs to take. Action-forward, practical, empowering.
20
Urgency / Scarcity Build
Real reasons why waiting costs something. Not fake countdown — actual stakes.
21
Myth-Busting Deep Dive
"You think [wrong belief] but actually [truth]." Frees viewer from false constraint.
22
Behind-the-Scenes Look
Insider perspective not normally visible. Feeling of exclusive access even in a public ad.
23
Vulnerability / Confession
Share struggles and breakthrough. Being imperfect and honest is the persuasion.
24
Logic / Rational Argument
Systematic case with data and cause-effect. For analytical decision-makers.
25
Sensory / Experiential Description
Paint a vivid picture of the experience. Activates imagination before action.
26
Risk Reversal
"What's the worst that could happen? You check and it takes 90 seconds." Reduce the downside.
27
Authority Stacking
Layer credibility — credentials, years, specific results. Stack until doubt dissolves.
28
Common Mistakes Breakdown
Name the specific errors viewer is probably making right now. Naming their mistakes builds trust.
29
Future-Pacing Vision
Detailed picture of life after taking action. Mind begins wanting what it can clearly picture.
30
Relatability Building
"I know you know this feeling..." Identification precedes persuasion.
31
Value Stacking
Accumulate multiple value propositions until the offer feels undeniable.
32
Simplicity Emphasis
"You just enter your zip. That's it." Every complexity removed lowers the action barrier.
33
Investment Justification

34
Exclusivity / FOMO
"This is only for people who [qualifier]." Exclusivity creates desire even for broadly available offers.
35
Community / Belonging
"Thousands in [state] already did this." Social belonging is a fundamental driver.
36
Challenge Overcome
How a specific difficulty was conquered. Creator's challenge mirrors viewer's hesitation.
37
Results Documentation
Show proof through specific numbers or evidence. Not claims — receipts.
38
Lifestyle Integration

🎥 Camera Direction Library — 35 Shot Types
Shot choice sets the emotional register. Match camera direction to format and energy. Wrong shot = wrong feel. These are the building blocks of visual grammar for UGC video production.
1
Direct to camera, static
Locked off tripod. Direct eye contact. Authoritative, composed, professional trust. Best for Polished Testimonial and Tutorial formats.
2
Direct to camera, handheld
Subtle movement from held phone or handheld rig. Intimate, authentic, slightly vulnerable. The slight drift says "real person, not professional shoot."
3
Handheld tracking (follow shot)
Camera follows subject walking. Kinetic energy, documentary feel, cinema verité authenticity. Best for Day-in-Life and Street Interview formats.
4
Low-angle looking up
Camera positioned below eye level shooting upward. Power and authority. Makes subject appear dominant, confident, larger than life. Good for authority personas.
5
High-angle looking down
Camera above eye level shooting down. Creates vulnerability and intimacy. Viewer feels protective toward subject. Good for empathy hooks and confession formats.
6
Eye-level neutral
Camera at exact eye height. Balanced, peer-to-peer, no hierarchy. Trust without authority. Default for most UGC formats. The invisible baseline.
7
Over-the-shoulder (OTS)
Camera positioned behind and slightly to the side. Viewer is beside the subject, sharing their perspective. Good for phone screen moments and discovery scenes.
8
POV (point of view)
Camera IS the viewer's eyes. Maximum immersion. Used in Phone Screen Recording format. Creates the feeling of personally discovering the offer.
9
Close-up on face (tight)
Frame from shoulders up, face fills most of frame. Emotional intensity, read every micro-expression. Used for peak emotional moments and reaction shots.
10
Medium shot (chest up)
Frame from mid-chest upward. Standard interview framing. Professional, versatile, safe. The workhorse shot for most testimonial formats.
11
Full body / wide shot
Frame shows full character from head to feet. Establishes location and character simultaneously. Good for opening establishing shots and lifestyle contexts.
12
Two-shot (interviewer + subject)
Two people in frame simultaneously. Used for Street Interview format. Social proof framing — the dynamic between two people is what registers as real.
13
Screen recording POV
Phone or computer screen fills the entire frame. Used for Phone Screen Recording format. App walkthrough, comparison result, application completion. No face required.
14
Selfie angle (held phone)
Creator holds phone at arms length, classic selfie composition. Authentic mobile-native feel. Creator economy visual language — implies casual, unplanned recording.
15
Mirror selfie
Camera captures creator via mirror reflection, often in bathroom or bedroom. Maximum home authenticity. Low-production signal is the feature, not the bug.
16
In-car dashboard mount
Camera mounted on dashboard or held from driving position. Commute content, roadside confessional register. Best for auto insurance and day-in-life content.
17
Hidden camera aesthetic
Framing and quality simulates surveillance or accidentally-left-on camera. Very low production value signals maximum authenticity. Best for DR Hook format.
18
News anchor frame (wide desk shot)
Creator seated at a desk with visible work surface, slightly wider than standard. Authority and information delivery mode. Used for Breaking News format.
19
Walking shot (self-filmed from hip)
Creator films themselves while walking, camera held at hip level pointed upward. In-motion authenticity, going somewhere energy. Best for Day-in-Life format.
20
Sitting / relaxed wide
Creator clearly settled and comfortable — couch, porch chair, kitchen table. The physical relaxation signals: this is their real space, this is a real conversation.
21
B-roll cutaway (no face)
Close or wide shots of environment elements, objects, and actions with no creator face. Visual evidence and lifestyle montage. Supports Mini-Documentary and Voiceover formats.
22
Extreme close-up (ECU)
Fills frame with one detail: hands on a document, phone screen showing a rate, a policy letter being opened. Creates visual proof of the specific moment being described.
23
Split-screen (side by side)
Frame divided into two panels, each showing different content simultaneously. Before/after, old/new, cost comparison. Used in Split-Screen Comparison format.
24
Talking head with background action
Creator in foreground, real activity happening in background: kids playing, traffic passing, TV on. Depth signal — the world continues around the creator, they're just pausing to share.
25
Reaction cam (watching something)
Creator looking at something off-frame and reacting — the viewer never sees what they're watching. The face is the content. Used in Reaction Cam format.
26
Candid / documentary observational
Camera positioned as if it captured the moment without being set up. Subject appears unaware or only slightly aware of being filmed. Maximum authenticity signal.
27
Time-lapse or fast-cut montage
Multiple rapid shots compressed. Shows passage of time, completion of a process, busy life context. Good for establishing the character's full life before the coverage moment.
28
Text-only with ambient background
No face in frame — just environment and text overlays. Creator voice is heard but not seen. High novelty pattern interrupt. Best for Text-on-Screen Native format.
29
Green screen / keyed background
Creator keyed over a virtual background. Location flexibility without location shoot. Used for Green Screen format. Lighting must match — edges must be clean.
30
Drone/establishing aerial
Wide overhead shot of neighborhood, city, or landscape. Cinematic scale for opening B-roll only. Establishes geographic context and scale. Use only in Mini-Documentary format.
31
Product/document close-up (tabletop)
Camera shoots down at a surface showing insurance card, policy document, phone screen, or paperwork. Physical evidence visible. Anchors the abstract claim in a tangible object.
32
Profile shot (side view)
Camera positioned at 90 degrees to creator's eyeline. Contemplative, narrative, cinematic. Used in Flashback format for "memory mode" moments where creator is in retrospective thought.
33
Bokeh background (blurred background)
Creator in sharp focus, background heavily blurred. Foreground subject pops against soft environment. Polished feel without requiring a clean background. Good for Polished Testimonial.
34
Dutch angle (slight tilt)
Camera tilted 5-15 degrees off-level. Tension, unease, urgency signal. Should be used sparingly — once per video maximum. Best for DR Hook's peak urgency moment.
35
Zoom push-in (slow zoom during key line)
Gradual slow zoom toward subject during delivery of most important claim. Emphasis on the line without a cut. Creates subconscious "lean forward" viewer response.

⚡ Energy & Performance Library — 25 Styles
How the character delivers. Same script at different energy levels produces completely different ads. Direct the performance, not just the words. Energy is a specific, directable quantity — not vague.
1
100% Energy
High-octane, urgent, fast pace, zero pauses, maximum intensity. DR Hook format only. Unsustainable for longer formats — but perfectly calibrated for 15-second pattern interrupts.
2
85% Energy
Confident and direct with slight urgency. Conversational authority — sounds like someone who knows and wants you to know. Versatile: works for DR Hook, UGC Testimonial, countdown formats.
3
75% Energy
Warm and deliberate with natural pauses. Most versatile energy level. Default for UGC. Feels like a real person having a real conversation — not performing, just sharing.
4
60% Energy
Calm and measured with professional trust. No urgency, no excitement — just clear delivery of important information. Default for Polished Testimonial. Conveys stability and credibility.
5
45% Energy
Quiet authority with slow pace and heavy emotional weight. Used for FE death-adjacent topics and Medicare retirement reflection. Silence between words carries meaning at this level.
6
Confessional whisper
Leaning slightly in toward camera, lower volume, secret being shared between two people. The physical lean plus reduced volume creates an intimacy that regular delivery can't achieve.
7
Reluctant expert
Clearly knows more than they're saying. Holds back just enough. Makes the viewer want to lean in for more. "There's a reason I'm bringing this up and you need to hear it."
8
Just got home from work
Fatigued authenticity. End-of-shift candor — the guard is down, the filter is off, they're just talking. Unpolished but real. Works especially well for working-class personas.
9
Morning before coffee
Unguarded, no performance, pure authenticity. Slightly slow, slightly squinty, clearly just woke up. The most believable state for "I just found this" hook styles.
10
Excited but containing it
Trying to stay calm about genuinely good news. The containment of visible excitement creates curiosity — viewer wants to know what's so exciting that they're actively trying not to show it.
11
Slightly embarrassed admission
Self-deprecating, humanizing, "I should have done this sooner" energy. The vulnerability of admitting a mistake makes the creator trustworthy — they're not boasting, they're confessing.
12
Righteous frustration
Controlled anger about something unfair. Industry exposé energy. "I'm telling you this because they won't." Clear emotional advocacy without tipping into rant territory.
13
Warm storyteller
Porch swing pace. Unhurried. Meandering toward the point. The listener trusts they're going somewhere because the warmth is genuine. Best for Flashback and FE formats.
14
Direct and no-nonsense
Wastes no words. Trusts the viewer to keep up. No softening, no hedging, no filler. Best for specific number openers and loyal customer personas who prize efficiency.
15
Teaching mode
Patient, clear, assumes the viewer doesn't know this yet. Measured pace with natural pauses for comprehension. Best for Tutorial format. Makes the creator the trusted expert.
16
Friend-to-friend
Low stakes, casual, "since you asked" energy. Feels like advice over coffee, not advice on a stage. No performance layer. The opposite of broadcast energy.
17
Community elder
Slow, weighty, every word considered, speaks as representative of a group. Not speaking to individuals — speaking to "people like us." Best for FE and Medicare community personas.
18
Nervous authenticity
Slight stumbling, real hesitation, not polished but believable. The imperfection IS the proof of authenticity. Particularly effective for first-time sharers who "don't usually post stuff like this."
19
Professional off-duty
Doctor, lawyer, nurse, financial advisor — clearly at home or in casual context, more candid than they'd be at work. The contrast between professional identity and casual setting creates trusted informality.
20
Quietly proud
Not boasting, just satisfied. "I handled my business" energy. No bragging, no performance — just someone who did the right thing and wants to acknowledge it calmly. Best for FE seniors.
21
Concerned for the viewer
Caring urgency. "I need you to hear this" not "buy this." The concern is for the viewer, not for the creator. Best for adult-child-for-parent formats and consequence warning hooks.
22
Reluctant advertiser
"I don't usually post stuff like this but..." frame. The meta-awareness of the ad context is used to defuse it. The hesitation to share is the strongest signal that the share is genuine.
23
Matter-of-fact reporting
No emotional manipulation, pure information delivery. Flat affect with occasional dry delivery. "The number was $67. That's it." Letting the fact do the work without performance amplification.
24
Gentle insistence
Soft but firm. Warm but not backing down. "I really need you to hear this." The combination of gentleness and persistence creates a sense of caring that hard sells can't match.
25
Surprised by the result
Genuine discovery energy. Recreating the original moment of finding out. Slight disbelief still present. The surprise is the authenticity signal — something that wasn't expected but is real.

How to Build an Input Prompt — Step by Step
The input prompt is a single paragraph that gives the video generation system everything it needs. Follow this formula.
Field 1
[Age + Ethnicity + Gender]
Be specific. "63 year old Black woman" not "older woman." Age range OK: "45-52 year old."
Field 2
[Accent]
Always specific. "warm Georgia accent" not "Southern accent." Pull from the Accent Library.
Field 3
[Wardrobe — 3 specific items]
"wearing a comfortable floral blouse, reading glasses, and a cardigan" — three specific pieces minimum.
Field 4
[Location + Action — specific]
"sitting at her kitchen table with bills and family photos on the wall behind her" — location + what they're doing + what's in the environment.
Field 5
[Camera Notes — optional but powerful]
"handheld medium frame with slight natural drift" or "locked-off medium shot with window light from left" — tells the system how to shoot it.
Field 6
[Energy + Physical Behavior]
"genuine relieved energy at 72%" or "direct intense eye contact at 88%, leaning slightly toward camera."
Field 7
saying "[Hook + Body + CTA as natural dialogue]"
Write it as natural speech, not ad copy. Hook → discovery/evidence → offer → CTA. No bullet points — one flowing natural statement.
Complete Example
"63 year old Black woman with a warm Georgia accent wearing a comfortable floral blouse and reading glasses sitting at her kitchen table with bills and family photos on the wall behind her, handheld medium frame with slight natural drift, genuine relieved energy at 72%, saying "I was sitting here stressing about what my kids would have to deal with when I'm gone and I found this. Part 5 · Platform 1
📘 Facebook & Meta — Platform Notes
The highest-performing platform for insurance verticals. Older audience, longer attention span, highest intent. Medicare and Final Expense dominate here. The audience is willing to watch 15-30 seconds if the content feels relevant and trustworthy.
Best Formats for Facebook
  • 1. Polished Testimonial — trust register works for 45-70 age demo
  • 2. Before/After — emotional arc resonates with mature audience
  • 3. UGC Testimonial — full-stack format holds attention
  • 4. Voiceover B-Roll — no-face format performs well in feed
  • 5. Compilation — rapid social proof for retargeting
Technical Specs
  • Aspect ratio: 9:16 (Stories/Reels) or 4:5 (Feed) or 1:1 (square feed)
  • Caption approach: Always on. 85% of Facebook video watched at zero volume
  • Hook timing window: 3 seconds to stop the scroll in Feed; 2 seconds in Reels
  • CTA style: Soft-medium. "Tap the link in the comments" performs best
  • Audience age sweet spot: 50–72 (Medicare/FE), 28–55 (Auto), 22–45 (ACA)
What Works on Facebook
  • Longer creative (15–45 seconds) outperforms short-form for 55+ audience
  • Real locations and real-feeling environments perform much better than studios
  • Warm, natural lighting outperforms studio light for this audience
  • Peer-to-peer tone works best — neighbor informing neighbor, not presenter presenting
  • Specific numbers in the first 3 seconds consistently improve CTR
  • Comments-as-CTA is more native than swipe-up or button-CTA for older audiences
What to Avoid on Facebook
  • Avoid TikTok-native editing (rapid cuts, trending audio) — alienates the core Facebook audience
  • Avoid overly polished production — Facebook feed values authenticity over production value
  • Avoid "link in bio" phrasing — Facebook doesn't have a bio link; use comments or CTA button
  • Avoid very high energy formats (DR Hook) for 65+ audiences — they disengage
  • Avoid small or light captions — this audience may have visual impairments; bold and large works
Facebook-Specific Prompt Note
When writing prompts for Facebook-targeted creative, add to the prompt: "slightly wider frame to allow for 4:5 crop" and "captions will be added in editing — leave natural pauses for readability." The Medicare and FE verticals should specify wardrobe that photographs as "respectable but approachable" — too casual loses trust; too corporate loses authenticity. The sweet spot for this platform: someone who looks like they could be your neighbor, but clearly has their life together.
Part 5 · Platform 2
🎵 TikTok — Platform Notes
Higher energy, younger skew, shorter attention window. ACA and Auto Insurance perform best here. The algorithm rewards novelty and watch time. The hook window is 1.5 seconds — not 3. Everything must be natively formatted for vertical scroll.
Best Formats for TikTok
  • 1. Text-on-Screen Native — built for TikTok's default zero-sound viewing
  • 2. Raw UGC — highest native feel for the platform
  • 3. DR Hook — pattern interrupt works with short attention window
  • 4. Reaction Cam — reaction content is deeply TikTok-native
  • 5. Phone Screen Recording — screen recordings feel completely native
Technical Specs
  • Aspect ratio: 9:16 only — no exceptions. No black bars.
  • Caption approach: On-screen text overlays (CapCut style) throughout entire video
  • Hook timing window: 1.5 seconds — if they're not stopped by then, they're gone
  • CTA style: Hard and direct. "Link in bio" or "Follow for more" but text overlay required
  • Audience age sweet spot: 18–34 for ACA; 22–45 for Auto; 50+ exists but smaller
What Works on TikTok
  • Native editing style (CapCut text, transitions, trending sounds used as secondary audio)
  • Creator appears to be filming casually — tripods feel too polished
  • Strong text hook in the first frame before creator even speaks
  • Watch time is the algorithm signal — formats that tease the conclusion work best
  • Reaction and discovery formats feel the most organic
  • Short comment-bait at the end ("comment your monthly rate 👇") drives engagement signals
What to Avoid on TikTok
  • Avoid slow openers — if the first second is quiet or empty, the viewer is already gone
  • Avoid Facebook-style "neighbor informing neighbor" pace — too slow for the TikTok feed
  • Avoid landscape or square crops — 9:16 full-bleed only
  • Avoid formal wardrobe and studio settings — too corporate for this platform
  • Avoid over-65 primary demographics as the target — ACA and Auto convert better
TikTok-Specific Prompt Note
For TikTok-targeted prompts, specify: "handheld selfie angle, 9:16 full bleed" and "creator at 80%+ energy, snappy pacing, no breath pauses longer than 0.5 seconds." Add text overlay instructions: "Bold white text with colored highlights for key numbers. Every number appears as text simultaneously with speech." The ACA vertical's best TikTok angle is the price-reveal: enter the calculator, see the result, react. 12–15 seconds. No longer.
Part 5 · Platform 3
📸 Instagram — Platform Notes
Dual-format platform: Feed (visual quality matters most) and Reels (more TikTok-like). Instagram skews slightly older than TikTok and more aesthetically conscious. All four verticals can work here — ACA and Auto for Reels, Medicare and FE for Feed and Stories targeting.
Best Formats for Instagram
  • 1. Branded Lifestyle — Instagram's native format, visually polished
  • 2. Text-on-Screen Native — works for Reels zero-sound viewing
  • 3. Day-in-Life — natural lifestyle integration performs in Feed and Stories
  • 4. Reaction Cam — high-emotion moments perform in Reels
  • 5. UGC Testimonial — credibility plus authenticity for 28–45 demo
Technical Specs
  • Aspect ratio: 9:16 for Reels/Stories; 4:5 for Feed; 1:1 for square feed
  • Caption approach: Reels — auto-captions on; Feed — captions optional but recommended
  • Hook timing window: 2 seconds for Reels; Feed can hold 3–4 seconds if image quality is strong
  • CTA style: "Link in bio" is native to Instagram — works without friction
  • Audience age sweet spot: 25–45 (ACA/Auto); 45–65 (FE); 55-70 (Medicare via Reels targeting)
What Works on Instagram
  • Visual quality matters more than Facebook — slightly better lighting and framing is rewarded
  • "Link in bio" is a completely native action for Instagram users — no friction
  • Aesthetic congruence — the creator's look should match the platform's visual standards
  • Story series (multiple 15-second stories) can tell a longer narrative than a single Reel
  • Saves and shares are the key algorithm signal — content that is useful to share performs best
  • The Branded Lifestyle format feels the most native to the platform's visual culture
What to Avoid on Instagram
  • Avoid the same video you made for Facebook — Instagram users immediately recognize repurposed content
  • Avoid heavy sales language in the caption — Instagram culture is allergic to overt ad copy
  • Avoid low-quality lighting — Instagram penalizes grainy, poorly-lit video in the Reels algorithm
  • Avoid long-form content in Reels — 15 seconds is ideal; 30 is acceptable; 45+ loses the algorithm
  • Avoid out-of-date aesthetic choices — what felt native in 2021 flags as old content in 2025
Instagram-Specific Prompt Note
Instagram prompts should specify: "slightly cleaner framing than raw UGC — creator appears polished but genuine" and "soft natural light or ring light at low intensity." The Branded Lifestyle format should include a specific lifestyle detail that photographs well: coffee cup with latte art, clean kitchen counter, visible plants or natural textures. The aesthetic detail does credentialing work on Instagram that the words do on Facebook. ACA is the highest-converting vertical on Instagram for the 22–35 freelancer demographic.
Part 5 · Platform 4
▶️ YouTube Shorts — Platform Notes
The highest average viewer intent of any short-form platform. YouTube viewers are in "find out" mode, not "scroll and discover" mode — they came to watch something. Shorts that feel like the beginning of a tutorial or a helpful explainer perform best. All four verticals work here.
Best Formats for YouTube Shorts
  • 1. Tutorial / How-To — YouTube audience is in learning mode; this is highest native fit
  • 2. Phone Screen Recording — POV walkthroughs are a YouTube Shorts staple
  • 3. Breaking News / Alert — "news you need to know" angle works with YouTube intent
  • 4. Polished Testimonial — higher trust register matches YouTube's authority culture
  • 5. Compilation — social proof works as proof-of-concept for intent-driven audience
Technical Specs
  • Aspect ratio: 9:16 required for Shorts feed; 16:9 plays as standard video, not Short
  • Caption approach: Auto-captions available; baked-in captions preferred for non-Shorts display
  • Hook timing window: 3 seconds — YouTube viewers are slightly more patient than TikTok
  • CTA style: "Link in description" works; "Comment below" drives engagement signals
  • Audience age sweet spot: 30–60 across verticals — broader age range than TikTok
What Works on YouTube Shorts
  • Educational framing ("here's how to...") feels completely natural here
  • Step-by-step structure with on-screen numbers works well
  • Authority signals (professional context, specific credentials mentioned) carry more weight
  • The audience trusts content that admits complexity — "this can seem confusing, here's how it works"
  • Subscribe hooks at the end have meaning here — people subscribe to YouTube channels
  • Comment sections drive discoverability — content that generates comments is boosted
What to Avoid on YouTube Shorts
  • Avoid pure entertainment energy — YouTube viewers came to learn something; reward that intent
  • Avoid content that assumes the viewer can swipe anywhere — they're watching a vertical video player
  • Avoid "link in bio" — YouTube doesn't have a bio in the same way; use "link in description"
  • Avoid content that can't stand alone — YouTube Shorts may be recommended to cold audiences across all ages
  • Avoid production quality that's too raw — YouTube has a slightly higher production expectation than TikTok
YouTube Shorts-Specific Prompt Note
YouTube Shorts prompts should specify: "slightly elevated production quality — clean background or intentionally organized space" and "direct to camera, eye-level, medium frame." The Tutorial and How-To format is uniquely powerful on this platform — add the phrase "teaching mode energy" to any Tutorial format prompt. The Medicare vertical's advisor-walkthrough format performs especially well: creator explains how the plan comparison works, step by step, in 45 seconds. The audience came to learn — let them.
Cross-Platform Anti-Fatigue Protocol
When running the same offer across multiple platforms simultaneously, use different formats per platform — not just different faces. Facebook: Polished Testimonial or Before/After. TikTok: Text-on-Screen or Raw UGC. Instagram: Branded Lifestyle or Day-in-Life. YouTube: Tutorial or Phone Screen Recording. This prevents the audience from seeing the same structural format on every platform, which signals ad oversaturation. Different format = different energy = different intent resonance = less fatigue across the funnel.
